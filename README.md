# Housefly-Mediated Antibiotic Resistance Analysis

## Project Overview
This project investigates the role of houseflies as carriers of antibiotic-resistant bacteria across different urban environments.

Samples were collected from **7 areas** and **3 zone types**:
- Residential Area  
- Street Food Area  
- Fish Market  

A total of **52 houseflies** were collected.  
From these, **363 bacterial colonies** were isolated.  
After PCR confirmation, **140 bacterial isolates** were analyzed using **Antibiotic Susceptibility Testing (AST)**.

This project combines **wet lab research + data analysis** to identify real-world resistance patterns.

## Objective
To analyze how **location, environment, and season** influence antibiotic resistance patterns in bacteria carried by houseflies.

## Dataset Structure
Each isolate was analyzed across multiple variables:

- Organism Name  
- Antibiotic Type (10 antibiotics tested)  
- Zone of Inhibition (mm)  
- Resistance Category (S / I / R)  
- Binary Resistance (0 / 1)  
- Area (7 locations)  
- Zone Type (Residential / Street Food / Fish Market)  
- Season (Summer / Winter)  
- Dilution Level (growth strength indicator)  


## Key Findings

- Antibiotic resistance was very high in several commonly used antibiotics.

- Ciprofloxacin and Meropenem showed higher sensitivity compared to others. These antibiotics are still relatively effective.

- Resistance varied across organisms. Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Vibrio spp. showed higher resistance patterns compared to others.

- Area-based variation was observed. Some locations showed consistently higher resistance levels, indicating possible environmental or human activity influence.

- Zone type comparison showed that street food and fish market areas had slightly higher resistance than residential areas.

- Seasonal variation was also observed. Resistance levels were higher in summer compared to winter, suggesting environmental factors may influence bacterial survival and resistance.

## Interpretation
The findings suggest that **houseflies act as mechanical vectors** of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

Their movement between waste, food sources, and human environments increases the risk of spreading resistant pathogens.

This has strong implications for:
- Public health  
- Food safety  
- Urban sanitation systems
